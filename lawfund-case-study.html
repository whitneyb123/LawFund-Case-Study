01 The Problem
Law school financial aid is a closed system for students who don't know how it works.

The students most likely to need scholarship money are the least likely to know they can negotiate for it — or how to do it professionally enough for a financial aid office to take seriously.

Before July 2026, a law student who couldn't cover their cost of attendance could borrow the remainder via Grad PLUS — up to the full COA. That safety valve is gone. The new $50K annual cap for JD students means every dollar above that limit must come from somewhere else: institutional scholarship, private loans at potentially high rates, or family money.

The problem compounds because law school award letters are opaque. Schools present aid packages in ways that obscure what's free money, what's a loan, and what the real 3-year cost is. A first-generation law student receiving their first offer has no reference point for what "good" looks like, no frame for what's negotiable, and no idea that financial aid offices respond to well-constructed appeals.

I built LawFund to solve three things in sequence: decode what an offer actually means, compare it against alternatives to identify leverage, and generate the appeal letter that most students don't know they're allowed to write.

This is an equity problem dressed as a financial problem. The students with access to law school consultants and financially sophisticated family networks know all of this. Everyone else is leaving tens of thousands of dollars on the table — or worse, taking on private loan debt they didn't know was avoidable.

02 The PM Decisions

Why it's built the way it is

Every structural decision was intentional. The things I didn't build matter as much as what I did.

D·01
Three connected stages, not a single tool
Decode → Compare → Appeal is a lifecycle, not three features. Each stage produces output the next stage uses. A student who only needs the appeal letter can skip to Step 3. A student who hasn't decoded their offer yet doesn't know what to put in an appeal. The flow is sequenced around the actual decision they're making, not around what's easiest to build.
Scope → lifecycle
D·02
The federal cap is baked into every calculation
The tool doesn't just show cost of attendance minus scholarship. It calculates available federal loans against the $50K/year cap and the student's existing undergrad debt against the $200K lifetime limit — then surfaces the real gap. This is the number that determines whether an appeal is necessary and how much to ask for. Most tools miss this entirely.
Precision → trust
D·03
Two separate AI calls on the appeal step
The appeal letter and the submission guidance are generated by separate API calls with different system prompts. One prompt is optimized for professional letter-writing with specific financial detail. The other is optimized for tactical advice: who to address, when to send, what most students forget. Combining them would produce a mediocre version of both. Separating them produces two excellent outputs.
Quality → two calls
D·04
The letter uses the student's actual numbers, not templates
Financial aid offices receive hundreds of appeal letters. The ones that work cite specific figures, reference the school's own aid packaging, and make a clear, quantified ask. Generic template letters get ignored. The system prompt explicitly instructs the model to reference the gap amount, the federal cap context, and the competing offer (if any) — because those specifics are what make the letter credible.
Specificity → effectiveness
D·05
No login, no data stored, no friction
A law student sharing their financial situation is sharing sensitive information — family income, hardship circumstances, debt levels. Adding any storage or account layer creates both a trust barrier and a real privacy obligation. The tool works immediately, collects nothing, and stores nothing. That's appropriate for v1, where the goal is to get people to use it — not to build a database.
Trust → zero storage
D·06
Need-based hardship appeals only — not merit negotiation
Merit negotiation (using a competing offer to extract more money from a higher-ranked school) is a real strategy — and the tool supports competing offer comparisons. But the appeal generator is scoped to need-based hardship because that's the most underserved scenario, the highest-stakes, and the one where a well-written letter makes the biggest difference. Trying to serve every appeal type in v1 would produce a worse tool for all of them.
Focus → depth

Why deterministic logic is separated from AI

The federal cap calculation and funding gap are not AI problems — they are arithmetic. Letting the model compute dollar amounts introduces hallucination risk and makes results non-reproducible. Deterministic logic runs first and produces exact numbers; the AI receives those numbers as structured input and handles only what it is actually good at: plain-language explanation, comparison framing, and persuasive letter drafting. This separation means the calculator is always correct even if the AI response is unavailable.

Observability and safety

Every key user action — offer decoded, comparison run, appeal generated — fires a named event for analytics. The serverless proxy logs Anthropic errors server-side without exposing them to the client, rate-limits by IP to prevent abuse, and enforces a payload size cap so prompt injection via large inputs is not viable. Disclaimers are rendered at each AI output point to ensure users understand the tool is informational, not legal or financial advice, and that the appeal letter should be reviewed and edited before sending.

HONEST ACCOUNTING

Limitations & Assumptions

What the calculator models

Federal loan availability under the One Big Beautiful Bill's $50K/year JD cap, offset by existing undergraduate debt toward the $200K lifetime limit. Annual and three-year gap estimates based on user-entered COA and scholarship figures.

What it does not model

Institutional loan programs, state grants, employer benefits, bar exam loans, or living expense variability. Part-time enrollment, joint degree programs, and transfer students are out of scope for v1.

What the AI does

Explains costs in plain language, frames negotiation positioning between two offers, and drafts a professional appeal letter using the student's specific numbers and hardship context.

What the AI does not do

Guarantee appeal outcomes, predict financial aid office decisions, or account for school-specific policies. Output quality depends on the specificity of what the user enters — vague inputs produce generic letters.

Numbers and policy details should be verified directly with your financial aid office before making enrollment decisions. The Big Beautiful Bill's provisions take effect July 2026 — confirm current status with your school.

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR SQUARE

The Pattern That Connects

LawFund runs on a decisioning pattern that shows up everywhere in commerce infrastructure: take messy, unstructured inputs from a stressed user — an award letter full of jargon, a competing offer with buried terms — run them through deterministic rules that produce exact, reliable state, then use AI to explain that state in plain language and generate a ready-to-use action.

That's not unique to financial aid. It's how a seller making sense of a chargeback dispute works. It's how a small business owner reading a loan offer works. It's how anyone who is not a specialist tries to navigate a system designed by specialists works. The inputs are messy. The rules are knowable. The gap is explanation and action — and that's where AI earns its place.

Square builds financial infrastructure for people who don't have a CFO. LawFund is the same pattern applied to people who don't have a financial aid consultant. The product instinct is identical: find the information asymmetry, model it precisely, and build the tool that closes it.
